# Module 2: Research Workflows

Duration: 15 minutes

## Goal

Use an AI agent to turn a broad research topic into a structured, source-grounded research workspace.

By the end of this module, you should have a small research workspace inside your personal `atendee-workspace/first-last/` directory.

You will create:

- `research-workflow.md`
- `sources/`
- `notes/`
- `index.md`
- `log.md`

## Step 1: Choose a Topic

Choose a research topic you want to explore during the workshop.

Your topic can be connected to:

- Your RET research experience.
- A STEM topic you teach.
- A question about AI, research, data, or education.

Example topics:

- AI agents for literature review.
- Climate data analysis.
- Air quality and public health.
- Robotics or sensor data.
- Cybersecurity research workflows.
- Materials science data.
- Research reproducibility.
- Agentic AI in scientific writing.

## Step 2: Refine the Topic

Ask your agent to help turn your broad topic into possible research questions.

Example prompt:

```text
My topic is [your topic]. Help me turn this into 3 focused research questions. For each question, explain what would make it researchable and what might make it too broad or too vague.
```

Choose one draft research question to use for the rest of the module.

## Step 3: Build a Search Plan

Ask your agent to create search terms and source criteria for your research question.

Example prompt:

```text
For this research question, create a search plan with keywords, related terms, source types to look for, inclusion criteria, and exclusion criteria. Keep the plan focused enough for a short workshop research activity.
```

## Step 4: Set Up Your Research Workspace

Ask your agent to create a small source-grounded research workspace inside your personal workshop directory.

Example prompt:

```text
Inside my workspace, set up a small research workflow for my topic. Create research-workflow.md, sources/, notes/, index.md, and log.md. Use this as a source-grounded research workspace: raw sources go in sources/, structured notes go in notes/, index.md tracks what exists, and log.md records changes. Do not use Obsidian-specific features or personal knowledge-base assumptions.
```

Your workspace should include:

```text
research-workflow.md
sources/
notes/
index.md
log.md
```

## Step 5: Create a Source Table

In `research-workflow.md`, create a source table with these columns:

| Citation or Link | Source Type | Main Claim | Evidence Used | Relevance | Limitations | Verification Status |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Use this table to track sources as you find them.

## Step 6: Add Initial Notes

For each useful source, ask your agent to create a short note in `notes/`.

Each note should include:

- Source title or citation.
- Main idea.
- Key evidence.
- How it relates to your research question.
- Limitations or concerns.
- Questions to follow up on.

## Step 7: Update the Index and Log

Ask your agent to keep `index.md` and `log.md` current.

Use `index.md` to list the files in your research workspace with one-line descriptions.

Use `log.md` to record what changed, what was checked, and what still needs attention.

## Quick Checkpoint

Be ready to show:

- Your research question.
- Your `research-workflow.md`.
- One source or candidate source.
- One thing the agent suggested that you still need to verify.

## Completion Check

Before moving on, confirm that you have:

- A focused draft research question.
- A search plan.
- A `research-workflow.md` file.
- A `sources/` directory.
- A `notes/` directory.
- An updated `index.md`.
- An updated `log.md`.
- At least one source or candidate source recorded.
